plantbot

If Androids Dream of Electric Sheep... do plants ponder the prospect of world domination?

The Plantbot enhances the abilities of regular house plants, allowing them to move towards sunlight and demand to be watered, capped by a real cheeky personality.

Inspiration

After watching the first Blade Runner film, I became obsessed with the genre of sci-fi and the idea of a dystopian future.

Learning Arduino

This was the project that first introduced me to the world of microprocessors, and how I could begin integrating my education in engineering and design.

I had to first learn how to use the Spark Photon, a lightweight IoT microprocessor similar to the Arduino. I began by isolating the robot's desired functionality, reading online documentation and testing individual components. After figuring out each of the features, I combined all of them into one circuit running off of the microprocessor.

components
  1. Spark Photon
  2. 4 x photocells
  3. 2 x DC motors
  4. motor driver
  5. moisture sensor
  6. speaker
  7. microSD card reader
  8. LED strip
  9. lithium battery and driver

Form Exploration

At this point, I had basically the neural network and insides of the robot, but no skeleton or body. This was the first time I had to design around physical components, as parts had to fit perfectly into eachother, and I also needed to take into account the resolution of the 3D printer. A clever feature I implemented was the removable pot, with leads running down the side into the soil. This allowed the moisture sensor to read the soil moisture, but more importantly, it could then detect when the pot was removed or inserted. It's a small design feature that required both a knowledge in the hardware, software, and physical form to execute.

Final

Through this project, I developed the skill to develop in parallel the software, hardware, and physical form of a product.

wobl bobl

Can machines create their own ecosystem without the need of human interference?

I wanted to tackle that question by creating robots that would interact on their own and generate ideas.

Research / Inspiration

I looked into Harvard's swarm robotics and Alonso Martinez's animated robots. I wanted the robots to be able to communicate wirelessly and autonomously, while possessing a charming personality.

IR Sending & Receiving

Arguably, the most difficult aspect of this project was figuring out the hardware. I decided to use infrared technology, as it was fast, low-fidelity, and would operate without needing the cloud.

Building The Robots

components
  1. 2 x adafruit feather
  2. 2 x IR emitter & receiver
  3. 2 x ping pong balls
  4. 2 x RGB LED diodes
  5. male to male jacks
  6. 2 x socks
  7. 2 x piezo buzzers
  8. 2 x transistors

First, I experiemted with a soft-bodied design, where the movement was driven by mini solenoids in the robots. Found materials, including socks, paper cups, scaffold wire, and ping pong balls were used in the body.

Deciding that the soft-bodied design was too fragile and prone to wires detaching, I moved onto creating a more rugged design. This one would trade movement with the option to toggle the settings with a built in button, which allowed for more thorough debugging and varied functionality.

Form Exploration

3D Printing & Assembly

Final

By creating two exact replicas, I wanted to practice refining my craft process by ensuring that all my decisions were carefully planned and calculated. If I were to wire one robot up this way, I'd do the same with the other. This project also taught me the difficulty that comes with working with hardware: debugging at times, would take a three-pronged approach that looked at my code, my circuits, and also the fidelity of the components themselves. I've found out that it isn't uncommon for sensors to be faulty out of the box!

As a demonstration, I set up the robots to send each other musical notes to play. If I had more time, I would've liked to make it more evident that information was actually being sent via IR, and apply some sort of music theory, so that the notes being sent were not just random.

"pass the salt, please"

How could one encourage communication between families?

Dinner table conversations were lackluster in my family. Delving deeper into the problem and searching for opportunities to intervene, I recognized the importance of salt & pepper shakers in American culture. No matter the drama boiling under the dinner table, there's still always room to ask your family to "pass the salt."

Ergonomic Study

We first had an exercise studying ergonomics and the hand. Using condoms full of plaster, we casted handles that we then sculted. Observing the resulting forms, I designed a handle that was informed by the ambidextrous handle. These concepts would later be translated into the form of my shakers.

Casting

I modeled and 3D printed the shaker, and built a three part mold out of plaster.

With slipcasting, the timing during each phase is absolutely crucial, ensuring that I achieve the desired thickness and have it come out of the mold intact. It takes around 45 minutes to make one, and I poured around 15 of them only to salvage 6 in the end.

After cleaning the shakers up and refining the surface, I poke holes into the top and send them to the kiln.

Final

After being fired in the kiln, the shakers shrink in size. Now they're smaller than the original 3D printed one. I've adjusted for the shrinkage, and printed a plug with an expressive mouth out of flexible resin. Users would be able to twist the plugs to change the expressions on the shakers.

The final pieces were dipped in clear glazer, and immediately wiped clean to leave only a thin layer of residue. The resulting product, surprisingly, took on a beautiful speckled matte finish.

moodboard

Could I design something that has limitless functionality, without the need of imbedded electronics?

The moodboard is an open ended calendar and journal, with wooden balls as its only means of storing information. Recognizing the lack of tactile response in the age of digital devices, it invites users to feel its interface and feedback.

Research / Inspiration

I knew I wanted to use balls as an integral part of the user interface. There's something about these round objects that bring out the playful curiousity in people; they beg to be picked up, felt, rolled. Most children's first toys are probably some form of ball. I wanted to build something that would communicate its functionality in a very universal yet open way, without the need of words or images.

There needed to be a way for users to log and display the information. I first looked into different types of physical calendars and journals. I also researched different types of marble machines, as I wanted the process of logging data with the balls to be rewarding and something to look forward to every day.

I initially wanted to create all these winding paths for the balls to roll down, and eventually have them fall into their designated boxes. However, the design became overly complicated, and I realized that I could strip down the complexity of the design and make it more intuitive by just allowing users to place the balls into the holes themselves. I shifted my focus to clarity and tactility. To reset the board, all the user had to do was lift the tab, and run his or her hand across all the balls, effectively popping them into the back, allowing them to fall down and roll back out onto the shelf.

Manufacturing the parts

I first designed the CAD in Rhino. There were four parts that would need to be made. Ideally, I would have made 3 of the complex parts on the CNC machine, and the hole-punched center sheet would be lasercut. However, I ended up having to build the bottom shelf by hand, due to the restrictions of the CNC machine. For the balls, I purchased wooden balls online and spray painted some of them blue and yellow.

xondas floating lamp

Brought on as a design intern, I had to build a product that would showcase the company's wireless power technology.

Xondas is a company on campus founded by a group of professors within the EE and Physics department. They wanted me to utilize magnetic levitation in the product to highlight the fact that they could this large capacity of power without anything touching. It was the first time I've worked on a project simultaneously with a group of engineers, and on a product that was meant to be operable and further developed for production. This meant that my work had to be ever more meticulous.

Brainstorming Functionality

After much debate and discussion, we came to the conclusion that, with their goal being to start a crowdfunding campaign, we'd want a slick product that would capture the target market of young tech-loving adults. I pitched many ideas that would utilize wireless power, including a living boardgame, floating lavalamp, and electronic building blocks. For the sake of simplicity, though, we decided to stick with a floating lamp. Although the idea itself wasn't entirely new, Xondas' technology allowed 10 watts to used to light an entire room, whereas in competitors only a dim LED could be powered. Specs like this, though, are hard to sell, and ultimately the product would have to stand out with its design.

Form Exploration

After experimenting with a levitating magnet kit, we found that by tweaking some settings, we could have the base float on an angle, exaggerating the effect of suspension. I went back to the drawing board, and slimmed down the light, too.

Continuing to think of the physical interface, I explored the possibility of the light being removeable. What if it could be carried around, placed on the wall, handled? Could it also be a light alarm?

3D Printing & Prototyping

We were first using a storebought kit to test the levitation. I had to revise my model to fit the circuit board.

Revisions & Iterations

I went back and polished up the design, adding in an interface for user interaction. I experimented with different patterns of metal wiring for touch control on the floating body.

I had to print several different iterations just to figure out how to fit the lid on correctly and secure the main circuit board in place.

We later redesigned our own circuit board, with a custom geometry so that the base would have as little of a footprint as possible. I also added a rotating base at the bottom.

3D print finished with matte coating and spraypaint.
